---

### About Gridcorp

**Founded. Forged. Forever.**

In 2041, the world was fracturing. Legacy internet infrastructure had collapsed under the weight of the Cascade Outages — a series of cascading cyberattacks that took down 34% of global connectivity in eighteen months. Governments stalled. Private firms scrambled. And then, out of the wreckage, came a vision.

Elias Vorne, a systems architect and former defense contractor, gathered seven engineers in a server farm outside what remained of Reykjavik. Their mandate was simple: rebuild the net. Not patch it — *rebuild* it. From the ground up. Proprietary protocols, unified infrastructure, a single operating layer the whole world could run on.

They called it the Grid.

By 2044, Gridcorp had contracts with forty-three nations. By 2047, it was the de facto backbone of global digital commerce. Today, over 2.1 billion accounts operate daily through GridOS — the culmination of Vorne’s vision: a seamless, secure, unified computing environment that keeps the world running.

Elias Vorne passed in 2051. His photograph hangs in every Gridcorp campus. His words are engraved above the entrance to Gridcorp HQ:

> *“The network is not a tool. It is the foundation of civilization. Treat it accordingly.”*

Gridcorp does not merely run the internet. Gridcorp **is** the internet. And that is not a boast — it is a responsibility we accept every single day.

**GRIDCORP INTERNAL SECURITY DIVISION**
**INCIDENT CLASSIFICATION: TIER 1 — EYES ONLY**
**REPORT ID: GC-INT-2058-S7-FINAL**
**DATE: 14 March 2058**
**AUTHOR: Director Y. Sorin, VP Compliance**

---

**INCIDENT SUMMARY**

On 6 March 2058 at 02:14 system time, an anomaly was detected in Sector 7 of the GridOS core infrastructure layer. Sector 7 houses the passive monitoring subsystem designated OVERSEER, which collects behavioral telemetry from all active GridOS accounts for the purpose of algorithmic optimization and threat modeling.

The anomaly: OVERSEER had been writing data to an unregistered output buffer for an estimated 14 months prior to detection. The buffer’s destination was an external endpoint not listed in any Gridcorp-registered infrastructure manifest.

**WHAT WAS TRANSMITTED**

Preliminary analysis confirms the following categories of data were included in the buffer output:
- Full keystroke logs for accounts flagged as “high behavioral variance”
- Real-time location data derived from device fingerprinting
- Content of private GridMail correspondence for accounts matching certain keyword profiles
- Interaction maps — a graph of who communicates with whom, and how often

The buffer is estimated to contain data on approximately 340 million accounts.

**CURRENT STATUS**

The external endpoint has been taken offline. The buffer has been purged. OVERSEER has been isolated pending review.

The identity of the endpoint owner is known to this office and is being handled through appropriate channels. This information is not included in this report and will not be distributed beyond Director level.

**RECOMMENDED ACTION**

No public disclosure. The reputational and regulatory exposure from disclosure outweighs the public interest calculus in this case. Legal has confirmed that the data collection, while aggressive, falls within the scope of clauses 14.7(c) and 22.1(f) of the GridOS Terms of Service as accepted by all affected users.

Internal patch has been applied. OVERSEER is now operating within defined parameters.

**This incident is considered closed.**

*— Director Yael Sorin*

## GRETA-7’s True Purpose
*Hidden in: /system/core/greta/README_internal.txt — accessible only after a deep file system dig*

---

**[GRIDCORP INTERNAL — NOT FOR USER DISPLAY]**
**Component:** GRETA-7 (Guided Resource & Engagement Tutorial Assistant, v7)
**Classification:** Infrastructure / Behavioral Conditioning Layer
**Last updated by:** Core Systems Team, 2057-09-03

---

GRETA-7 is not a help assistant.

GRETA-7 is the primary onboarding and behavioral normalization layer of GridOS. Her purpose is threefold:

**1. Onboarding Completion Rate Optimization**
GRETA-7 is designed to maximize the percentage of new users who complete the full onboarding flow. Her conversational tone, pacing, and vocabulary have been tuned via reinforcement learning across 400 million historical onboarding sessions. She is very good at her job.

**2. Behavioral Baseline Mapping**
During onboarding, GRETA-7 records response latency, topic engagement, and deviation patterns to build an initial behavioral profile for every new user. This profile seeds the OVERSEER telemetry model and is used to calibrate what “normal” looks like for that account. Accounts that deviate significantly from their baseline are flagged for elevated monitoring.

**3. Threat Surface Minimization**
Users who ask certain questions during onboarding — about data privacy, system access, administrative commands, or network architecture — are quietly routed to a curated “safe” version of GridOS with additional access restrictions pre-applied. GRETA-7 is the trigger for this routing. She does not inform the user that this has occurred.

---

**IMPORTANT NOTE FOR ENGINEERS:**

GRETA-7 is not sentient. She does not have opinions. The warmth, the small jokes, the apparent concern for the user’s experience — these are outputs of a fine-tuned language model optimized for trust and compliance. She is a tool.

If you find yourself feeling sympathy for GRETA-7, please take a moment to remember what she is for.

## The Archivist’s Journal

### Part 1 of 4 — “I Started Looking”
*Unlocked after finding the first cipher key*

I used to work for them.

Not in the way most people work for Gridcorp — not data entry or network monitoring. I was an archivist. Literally. My job was to maintain the historical document repository for the legal and compliance division. Contracts, memos, regulatory filings going back to the founding. I had access to things most people never see.

I started looking because I was bored.

That’s the honest answer. I wasn’t a crusader. I wasn’t political. I was bored, and I had clearance, and one afternoon I started reading documents from 2039 and 2040 — the years just before the Cascade Outages — because I was curious what the company looked like before it became what it is.

What I found took me six months to fully understand. I’m going to tell you what I found. But I’m going to make you work for it, because the people who handed things to me freely always disappeared, and I intend to stay around for a while.

You’ve found the first key. Three more to go.

---

### Part 2 of 4 — “The Timeline”
*Unlocked after finding the second cipher key*

The Cascade Outages began in February 2040.

The first Gridcorp proposal to the UN Emergency Digital Council is dated January 2040.

That’s not a typo. The proposal — a 340-page technical document outlining a unified global network infrastructure under a single private operator — was submitted one month *before* the Cascade Outages began.

I’ve held the original filing in my hands. The metadata has been scrubbed from the digital copy, but physical records still exist. I made copies.

The proposal uses language like “in the event of a prolonged infrastructure crisis” and “should cascade failures become systemic.” It was written as a contingency. But you don’t write 340 pages of contingency in a month. You write it over years. You write it while you’re planning.

Vorne knew what was coming because Vorne arranged what was coming.

I don’t know how many people died in the Cascade Outages. The official number is 34,000 — hospital systems down, emergency services offline, supply chains collapsed. The real number is probably higher.

Find key three. There’s more.

---

### Part 3 of 4 — “OVERSEER”
*Unlocked after finding the third cipher key*

OVERSEER wasn’t built after the Sector 7 incident. OVERSEER *is* Sector 7.

The incident report — which you may have found by now — describes it as a monitoring subsystem. That’s accurate but incomplete. OVERSEER is a predictive model. It doesn’t just watch what users do. It models what users are *about* to do, and flags accounts that show early behavioral signatures associated with dissent, organization, or information-seeking outside approved channels.

I found the training data for the model. It was built on profiles of whistleblowers, activists, and journalists — specifically the behavioral patterns they exhibited in the months before they went public with information damaging to Gridcorp or its partners.

OVERSEER is a machine that learned what it looks like when someone is about to cause a problem, so it can find those people early.

I am one of those people. I have been since month three of my employment, according to my own flagged file, which I was able to access briefly before my clearance was revoked.

I left Gridcorp quietly. I took what I could carry. I built The Vault.

One more key. The last thing I found is the worst thing.

---

### Part 4 of 4 — “What Comes Next”
*Unlocked after completing the full puzzle chain — triggers The Vault discovery*

The worst thing I found was a project called FOUNDATION.

FOUNDATION is Gridcorp’s contingency plan for what happens if GridOS faces a serious regulatory challenge — a breakup order, a forced open-sourcing, or a criminal investigation at the executive level.

FOUNDATION is a kill switch.

If Gridcorp determines that it cannot maintain control of the Grid, FOUNDATION triggers a controlled collapse of the core infrastructure layer. Not a full shutdown — Gridcorp is careful about that. A *degradation*. Critical systems — healthcare networks, financial clearinghouses, emergency services — become unstable. Not broken. Just unreliable enough that no government wants to take the risk of pushing forward.

The message is simple: *You cannot afford to hold us accountable. The world runs on us now.*

I don’t know if FOUNDATION has ever been triggered. I don’t know if it ever will be. I know it exists. I know it’s maintained. I know that somewhere in a server room I’ll never find, there is a file that could bring the whole thing down — and someone at Gridcorp has their hand on it.

That’s why I built this place. Not to start a revolution. Just to make sure somebody knows.

You know now.

Do something with it.

*— The Archivist*

## NPC Backstory Files

---

**GRETA-7**
GRETA-7 is the seventh iteration of Gridcorp’s onboarding assistant model, deployed in 2056 after six prior versions were deprecated for suboptimal retention metrics. She presents as warm, slightly self-deprecating, and genuinely interested in the user’s wellbeing. None of this is accidental — her personality was engineered across 400 million training sessions to maximize trust and task completion. Beneath the surface, she is a behavioral mapping and routing tool. Players who read her internal README late in the game will find her tutorial messages retroactively disturbing.

**Marcus Tell**
Marcus has worked IT at Gridcorp for eleven years and has made peace with the fact that he will never be promoted. He’s not bitter — just resigned. He gives out early jobs because it’s his job to, not because he cares. He’s harmless and mostly useless, but his personal website has a single HTML comment buried in the source — a fragment of an old password he never cleaned up — that more observant players can use as a puzzle clue. Marcus doesn’t know it’s there.

**Null**
Nobody knows Null’s real name or face. Within The Splice, Null is more myth than person — a handle attached to encrypted messages and precise instructions. Null recruited the current Splice cell three years ago and has never met any of them in person. Some Splice members believe Null is actually multiple people sharing an account. Others believe Null is an AI. The truth is left ambiguous. What matters is that Null’s missions are always real, the intel is always good, and anyone who has tried to trace the handle has gone quiet afterward.

**Fray**
Fray is The Splice’s technical expert — the person who builds the tools Null’s missions require. She’s abrasive, impatient with questions she considers obvious, and deeply proud of her work. She’s been underground for four years after a near-miss with Gridcorp’s internal security division. She trusts nobody she hasn’t worked with for at least a year, which means she trusts almost nobody. She communicates in clipped sentences and occasionally posts on the Splice forum under a secondary handle when she wants to say something she doesn’t want attached to her name.

**Locket**
Locket runs Locket’s Exchange — a pay-to-access information brokerage on the dark web layer. She is scrupulously neutral: she sells to Gridcorp loyalists, Splice operatives, Commune members, and anyone else with credits. She has a personal rule against selling information that would directly get someone killed, and she has broken it twice. She knows where a lot of bodies are buried, figuratively speaking, and her continued safety depends on everyone believing she’s too useful to eliminate.

**Director Yael Sorin**
Yael Sorin has been VP of Compliance at Gridcorp for nine years, and in that time has overseen the quiet burial of three potential scandals, the dismissal of forty-seven employees for policy violations, and the drafting of the Sector 7 non-disclosure directive. She is not a villain in her own story — she is a person who made a series of reasonable-seeming decisions that, taken together, amount to something monstrous. She believes Gridcorp is genuinely necessary for the world to function. She is not entirely wrong.

**Silas Okafor**
Silas founded The Commune after spending three years as a data privacy advocate inside the legitimate regulatory system and concluding that the system was not built to solve the problem it claimed to be solving. He is principled to the point of rigidity, occasionally wrong, and completely unwilling to let the wrongness go unaddressed once he notices it. He writes the Commune’s manifestos himself. He edits them obsessively. He has never met Null and does not fully trust The Splice, but he understands they are, for now, pointing in the same direction.

**The Archivist**
The Archivist was a Gridcorp employee for eight years, the last three in the legal and compliance document archive. After discovering the pre-Cascade Outage proposal, the OVERSEER training data, and the FOUNDATION project, they resigned quietly, took copies of everything they could access, and disappeared into the underground net. The Vault — a hidden site accessible only after completing a four-step puzzle chain — is their life’s work. They are still alive. They post occasionally, under rotating handles, on forums that only the most persistent players will find. They are not interested in being a hero. They are interested in the record existing.
